Human Performance & Crew Health

We see the problem forming before it becomes an emergency.

A crew far from Earth has no second chance and no nearby help. SSAI treats crew health as a data and modeling problem, fusing medical wearables, environmental sensors, and diagnostic imaging into real-time physiological situational awareness, so flight surgeons and crews act on a risk while there is still time to act.

The Capability

Physiological monitoring at scale.

We give flight surgeons and crews a continuous, fused picture of human health in the space environment, built from the same data discipline SSAI has applied to Earth and space science for nearly five decades.

A single waveform tells a flight surgeon very little. The signal is in the relationships: heart rate against carbon dioxide, sleep against cognitive performance, hydration against an exercise load and the cabin around it. We fuse medical wearables, environmental sensors, and diagnostic imaging into one Internet of Medical Things (IoMT) layer, then model it so a forming problem surfaces as an early, actionable signal rather than a late alarm.

This is the front end of SSAI's Health practice. It feeds the autonomous clinical decision support that acts when Earth is too far to ask, and it draws on the space-radiation and environmental modeling that defines the risk a crew is flying through. We build it the way we build every mission system: grounded in data, accountable for outcomes, ready the first time, and every time.

What We Do

From raw signal to a decision in time.

We treat the crew as an instrumented system. We capture the signals, fuse them into one picture, model the trajectory, and hand a flight surgeon something they can act on before the window closes.

01 Sensor Fusion

One IoMT layer for the crew and the cabin

We integrate medical wearables, environmental sensors, and onboard diagnostic imaging into a single Internet of Medical Things layer. The body and the cabin are read together, because the same carbon dioxide, radiation, and noise that load the vehicle load the crew inside it.

02 Situational Awareness

Real-time crew health, not a daily readout

We turn fused telemetry into continuous physiological situational awareness, a live picture a flight surgeon can hold in their head. Status is current, not a report from yesterday, so the crew and the ground share the same view of how the mission is acting on the people flying it.

03 Predictive Modeling

Early signals, not late alarms

We model the trajectory of a crew member's physiology against the space environment to surface deviation before it becomes an event. The goal is lead time: a forming problem seen early enough that a flight surgeon, or an autonomous system, still has a choice to make.

04 Human System Risk

NASA HRP risks as data problems

NASA's Human Research Program tracks 30+ Human System Risks, from cognitive and behavioral health to cardiovascular and sensorimotor adaptation. We address them as data and modeling problems, mapping each risk to the signals that reveal it and the model that forecasts it.

05 Diagnostic Imaging

Onboard imaging, read where it is taken

We bring diagnostic imaging into the fused picture and into reach of automated analysis, so an ultrasound or scan acquired in flight informs the crew-health model directly. The interpretation does not have to wait for a downlink to the ground.

Built on heritage

Earth-system rigor, turned inward

The data assimilation and physics-based modeling SSAI has delivered for NASA and NOAA for decades is the same discipline we now apply to the human body. We are not learning the method here. We are pointing it at the crew.

Why It Matters

Distance turns routine care into a self-contained problem.

The deep-space gap

When the nearest specialist is millions of miles behind you

On the International Space Station, a flight surgeon on the ground sees the crew in near real time. On Artemis and the road to Mars, that link stretches to a 1.3 second delay at the Moon and 4 to 24 minutes at Mars, and the crew operates largely on its own. The ground can no longer be the eyes on the patient minute to minute.

Crew-health monitoring is what closes that gap. By fusing the signals and modeling the trajectory on board, the system carries the situational awareness a distant flight surgeon used to provide, so the people on the mission, and the autonomous tools supporting them, are never reading the crew blind.
