Our Science

From observation to understanding

For more than forty years, SSAI has invested in the science behind Earth's complex systems and our place in the universe. We organize that work around three domains — the Sun, the Earth, and the universe beyond — and the discipline that ties them together: turning what the world's observing systems see into understanding on the ground.

Explore the domains Read the research

One question, asked three ways

Whether designing, building, maintaining, or upgrading ground- and space-based instruments — or managing, analyzing, and distributing the data they return — SSAI contributes to advances across the oceanic, terrestrial, atmospheric, biological, and astronomical sciences.

We do not own the satellites or build the flight hardware; NASA, NOAA, and their primes do. What SSAI brings is the science discipline around the measurement — engineering and integrating instruments, operating them on orbit, validating and calibrating what they return, and writing the algorithms and models that turn a raw signal into a number a scientist can trust. The same craft applies whether the instrument is pointed at the Sun, at the Earth below, or out toward the edge of the observable universe.

Domain 01 — The Earth

Earth Science — our largest practice

SSAI scientists contribute to nearly every NASA observing system measuring Earth's atmosphere, water, ice, land, and biological activity.

Our work spans remote sensing — from algorithm development through data validation, calibration, maintenance, and distribution — the observation and modeling of Earth-system components such as land surface and hydrology, the oceans, and the cryosphere, and geodesy and solid-Earth geophysics. We develop algorithms that detect and quantify water-related variables from satellite, airborne, and in-situ data, applying large-scale geospatial processing and high-performance computing to atmospheric correction and aquatic applications.

The view from above

A planet measured, layer by layer

Earth is the most heavily instrumented object in the solar system — and SSAI helps tend almost every layer of that watch, from the top of the atmosphere to the bed of the ocean.

Atmosphere, ocean, ice, land, and the living biosphere are each observed by their own constellation of instruments. The science is in stitching those measurements into one coherent picture of a system that never holds still.

Domain 02 — The Sun

Heliophysics — reading the space weather

SSAI studies the interaction of the solar wind with the upper atmosphere and the science of space weather — understanding and predicting solar activity and its interaction with Earth's magnetic field.

We support the development and operations of NAIRAS, the Nowcast for Aerospace Ionizing Radiation System developed at NASA Langley, which computes in real time the radiation experienced by the crews of aircraft and spacecraft from galactic cosmic rays, solar energetic particles, and the radiation belts. We also study variations in total solar irradiance as a fundamental forcing of Earth's climate system — the thread that ties the Sun directly back to the Earth domain.

Domain 03 — The Universe

Planetary Science & Astrophysics — to the edge of the observable

SSAI extends its science from the Moon to the edge of the observable universe.

We are developing HOLMS — the Heterodyne OH Lunar Miniaturized Spectrometer — destined for the lunar surface through NASA's Commercial Lunar Payload Services initiative, to measure and understand the water cycle in the Moon's thin atmosphere. That water will be crucial to Artemis, which aims to establish a permanent human presence on the Moon. Our instrument and engineering teams reach further still, into the astrophysics missions that map our place among the stars.

The connective tissue

Data science is where observation becomes knowledge

Across all three domains, the harder half of the work begins after the instrument returns its signal.

SSAI builds and runs the data systems and DAACs that archive and distribute mission data, develops the assimilation methods that fuse millions of observations into a single best estimate of a system's state, and applies AI and machine learning, modeling, and high-performance computing to problems that were intractable a decade ago. It is the discipline that lets a measurement of the Sun, the Earth, or a distant galaxy become a result a scientist anywhere can build on.

Scientific leadership

Science overseen by people who have done the science

SSAI's research is overseen by Chief Corporate Scientist Oreste Reale, Ph.D., with 25 years of NASA Goddard experience in atmospheric dynamics, hurricanes, and data assimilation.

Our scientists lead and staff NASA working groups, sit on mission science teams, and publish the peer-reviewed work that turns observation into understanding — including the team behind NASA's Black Marble nighttime-lights product, used to study disaster impact, urbanization, and human displacement. The MERRA-2 reanalysis earned a 2019 NASA Agency Honor Award. This is what distinguishes SSAI from a satellite operator: the wedge from observation to understanding runs straight through our people.
